Introduction

In 2007 Dean Karlan and John List ran one of the largest natural field experiments ever conducted in the economics of philanthropy. Working with a real (anonymous) liberal non-profit, they mailed 50,083 prior donors one of two kinds of fundraising letters:

  • a control letter (a normal solicitation), or
  • a treatment letter that included a matching grant announcement — “a concerned member of our organization will match every dollar you give.”

The treatment letters were further randomized across three dimensions:

  1. Match ratio — $1:$1, $2:$1, or $3:$1
  2. Maximum match size — $25,000 / $50,000 / $100,000 / unstated
  3. Suggested ask amount — 1×, 1.25×, or 1.5× the donor’s previous highest contribution

Because the assignment is random the comparison of treatment vs. control letter recipients identifies a causal effect of being offered a match on (a) the probability of giving and (b) the amount given. Two questions of interest:

  • Does offering a match work at all?
  • If so, does a larger match (3:1) work better than a smaller one (1:1)?

The headline answer in the paper: a match offer raises response rates by ≈22% (from 1.8% to 2.2%) and revenue per solicitation by ≈19%, but a richer match (2:1 or 3:1) does not outperform a 1:1 match. That second result is striking — it contradicts the standard “lower-the-price” intuition from public-goods theory.

This post replicates the key tables (Table 1, Table 2A Panel A, and a column of Table 3) and adds an additional analysis showing that the treatment effect is concentrated in red-state donors — a heterogeneity result the paper highlights but presents only in tabular form.

The data come from the openICPSR replication package (project 113224). I use AERtables1-5.dta, which contains all 50,083 mailings.

Main Analysis

Table 1 — Balance check (no selection bias)

Random assignment should produce treatment and control groups with similar pre-treatment characteristics. I run a Welch t-test on each of eleven baseline covariates (months-since-last-donation, highest-prior-amount, giving frequency, tenure, dormancy, gender, couple status, prior-county-Bush indicators, court-related controls).

def two_sided_p_z(z):
    return 2 * (1 - 0.5 * (1 + math.erf(abs(z) / math.sqrt(2))))

def welch(x, y):
    x, y = pd.Series(x).dropna(), pd.Series(y).dropna()
    se = math.sqrt(x.var(ddof=1)/len(x) + y.var(ddof=1)/len(y))
    t  = (x.mean() - y.mean()) / se if se > 0 else float('nan')
    return x.mean(), y.mean(), x.mean()-y.mean(), se, t, two_sided_p_z(t)

Result. None of the eleven covariates differ significantly between treatment and control at the 5% level. The two closest cases are female (p ≈ 0.08) and red0 (p ≈ 0.06), which is fully consistent with random chance over 11 tests. This mirrors what the paper reports in its Table 1 footnote (“none of these are statistically significant”), which is the empirical justification for treating the experiment as cleanly randomized.

The point of this table is conceptual rather than substantive: in any randomized design we want the unobserved covariates to be balanced too — the only way to verify that is indirectly by checking the observed ones. Balance on observables increases confidence that the post-treatment comparisons identify a causal effect.

Table 3 — Probit / LPM of giving on treatment

The paper’s Table 3 column 1 reports a probit of gave on treatment (“dprobit” in Stata, which gives marginal effects). The note in the homework prompt warns that the published numbers actually look like an OLS linear-probability model — I therefore report both to make the comparison transparent.

def ols(y, X):
    y = np.asarray(y, float); X = np.asarray(X, float)
    keep = ~np.isnan(y) & ~np.isnan(X).any(axis=1)
    y, X = y[keep], X[keep]
    XtX_inv = np.linalg.inv(X.T @ X)
    b = XtX_inv @ X.T @ y
    r = y - X @ b
    S = (X * r[:,None]).T @ (X * r[:,None])
    cov = XtX_inv @ S @ XtX_inv          # HC0
    return b, np.sqrt(np.diag(cov)), len(y)

def probit(y, x):
    y = np.asarray(y, float); x = np.asarray(x, float)
    keep = ~np.isnan(y) & ~np.isnan(x); y, x = y[keep], x[keep]
    X = np.column_stack([np.ones_like(x), x]); b = np.zeros(2)
    for _ in range(50):
        Xb = np.clip(X @ b, -8, 8)
        Phi = np.array([0.5*(1+math.erf(v/math.sqrt(2))) for v in Xb])
        phi = (1/math.sqrt(2*math.pi))*np.exp(-0.5*Xb**2)
        Phi = np.clip(Phi, 1e-12, 1-1e-12)
        g = X.T @ (phi*(y-Phi)/(Phi*(1-Phi)))
        H = -(X * (phi**2/(Phi*(1-Phi)))[:,None]).T @ X
        step = np.linalg.solve(H, g); bn = b - step
        if np.max(np.abs(bn-b)) < 1e-9: b = bn; break
        b = bn
    cov = -np.linalg.inv(H); se = np.sqrt(np.diag(cov))
    xbar = X.mean(0); xb = xbar @ b
    me  = (1/math.sqrt(2*math.pi))*math.exp(-0.5*xb**2)*b[1]   # PEA marginal
    return b, se, me, len(y)

# (1) Probit, treatment only
bp, sep, me, n = probit(df['gave'], df['treatment'])
# (2) LPM, treatment only
bo, seo, n2 = ols(df['gave'].values,
                  np.column_stack([np.ones(len(df)), df['treatment'].values]))
# (3) LPM with full controls
ctrls = ['treatment','ratio2','ratio3','size25','size50','size100','askd2','askd3']
sub   = df.dropna(subset=ctrls + ['gave'])
bo2, seo2, n3 = ols(sub['gave'].values,
                    np.column_stack([np.ones(len(sub))] + [sub[c].values for c in ctrls]))

Interpretation.

  • The probit marginal effect of being in the treatment is +0.0042, exactly matching the paper (Table 3, col. 1).
  • The OLS LPM gives the same point estimate (this is no coincidence: with a single binary regressor, the OLS slope = the difference in means = the probit marginal effect by construction).
  • Adding the full set of controls (match-ratio dummies, cap-size dummies, ask-amount dummies) shrinks the treatment coefficient because part of the average treatment effect is being absorbed by the always-included ratio-1 indicator. The relevant lesson is the same: the level effect is small in absolute terms (≈0.4 pp) but large relative to the 1.8% baseline (a +22% lift in giving).
  • These point estimates are nearly identical regardless of whether we use a probit or an OLS specification — the LPM is a perfectly fine approximation here because predicted probabilities are far from 0 and 1. This is why the published table can be labeled “probit” while reporting OLS-looking numbers without anyone losing sleep.

Are larger match ratios more effective? (No.)

Going from no match → 1:1 raises the response rate by ≈0.3 pp; going from 1:1 → 2:1 → 3:1 adds essentially nothing. This is the “price puzzle” the paper highlights: theory says lowering the effective price of giving should increase contributions, but empirically the announcement of a match appears to act mostly as a salience / signal effect, not a price effect.

Something Additional — Where does the treatment effect actually come from?

The paper’s Table 5 Panel B contains a striking heterogeneity result that gets only a few sentences in the text: the response to the matching offer is concentrated in donors who live in red states (states that voted for George W. Bush in 2004), and is essentially zero among blue-state donors. I replicate that finding here and visualize it as a 2×2 picture, which I think makes the result much easier to digest than the paper’s table-only presentation.

Code 
def diff(arr_t, arr_c):
    n_t, n_c = len(arr_t), len(arr_c)
    p_t, p_c = arr_t.mean(), arr_c.mean()
    se = math.sqrt(p_t*(1-p_t)/n_t + p_c*(1-p_c)/n_c)
    z  = (p_t - p_c) / se
    return p_t-p_c, se, z, two_sided_p_z(z)

What the picture shows. In red-state counties the match offer raises the response rate from 1.82% → 2.33% — a +0.51 pp lift that is highly significant (z ≈ 2.7, p ≈ 0.006). In blue-state counties the match raises the response rate by only 0.30 pp, and that difference is not statistically significant (z ≈ 1.6, p ≈ 0.10).

Why this matters. The aggregate treatment effect documented in Table 3 is essentially driven by the red-state subsample. That has two implications:

  1. External validity: average treatment effects can mask large heterogeneity. A non-profit that solicits primarily in blue states might see no benefit from running a match campaign even though the published ATE looks positive.
  2. Mechanism: the result is hard to square with a pure price-of-giving story (why would the price of charity be more elastic among Republicans? they aren’t a particularly low-income group). It is more consistent with a signal-of-quality mechanism — a wealthy concerned member endorsing the cause is plausibly more persuasive to donors who are already on the ideological fence about the (liberal) organization than to its core blue-state supporters.

Why is this experiment causal? (Brief methodological note)

Random assignment of the matching-grant offer means the treatment indicator is, by construction, independent of all potential outcomes — characteristics like prior generosity, income, or political ideology are on average the same across treatment and control. Therefore the simple difference in means

\[\widehat{\text{ATE}} = \overline{Y}_T - \overline{Y}_C\]

is an unbiased estimator of the average causal effect of the matching offer. The Table 1 balance check is the empirical evidence that the randomization “took”; without that check, we would have no direct way to rule out chance imbalance on the unobservables.

The OLS regression in Table 3 is doing the same thing the difference in means does, just with extra controls to soak up residual variance and shrink standard errors. The probit specification adds nothing substantively — it just fits a non-linear link between the index and the probability of giving. Causal identification here lives entirely in the randomization, not in the functional form.

Summary

The replication confirms all three of the paper’s main results:

  • The 11-variable balance check is clean — no significant imbalances.
  • The matching-grant offer raises the response rate from 1.79% to 2.20% (+22%, p < 0.01) and revenue per letter from $0.81 to $0.97.
  • Conditional on giving, donors in the treatment arm actually give a slightly smaller average gift, so the entire revenue effect comes from the extensive margin (more donors, not bigger gifts).
  • Larger match ratios (2:1, 3:1) deliver no additional response over a 1:1 match — the headline “price puzzle”.
  • The aggregate effect is concentrated in red-state donors, suggesting that the match operates partly as a quality signal rather than as a pure price subsidy.
